You are being asked to be a volunteer in a research study.

PURPOSE

The purpose of this study is

To document and analyze the practice of national-level Internet censorship through the creation of a global network of researchers running censorship measurement software. This measurement software will run tests from your computer or dedicated hardware device which attempt to access a list of sensitive URLs. The software will collect data from these tests and send it back to servers hosted at the University of Toronto which are maintained by the researchers. Data will be analyzed at Stony Brook University. These data will be used to determine what content is being censored, what technical methods are used for censorship and how censorship changes over time.

It is anticipated that there will be 15 subjects in the study overall. It is not anticipated that any will enroll at SBUStony Brook University. You are eligible to participate in this research if you have a computer that meets the software’s minimum requirements and you have signed this consent form.

PROCEDURES

If you decide to be in the study, your part will involve:

  • Downloading a software client and installing it on to your computer. This should take approximately 5 minutes and only needs to be done once.
  • Running the software on your computer. The software will run as a background process on your computer. It will run tests for network censorship which may take up to one hour, but do not require your interaction.
  • Uninstalling the software should you decide to no longer participate in the project.

RISKS / DISCOMFORTS

The following risks/discomforts may occur as a result of you being in this study:

  • Your ISP or network administrator may be able to see the list of websites being accessed by the measurement software. There is a risk that accessing such websites and running these tests violates network policies.
  • There is a risk that accessing these sensitive websites is in violation of local laws or regulations.

The potential risks posed by conducting these tests are highly specific to the context in which the tests take place. These risks are highly dependent on the domestic political and legal context in your country.

The data that the tool will collect are regularly transmitted on the Internet and the measurement tests do not compromise, circumvent or otherwise disrupt the regular functioning of the networks being probed. While we know of no explicit laws that forbid the collection of network measurements on Internet censorship, the nature of this research can potentially have political impacts and could be seen unfavorably by certain authorities. If an authority is sensitive to information of its censorship regime being exposed and views exposure as potentially damaging then nationals of that country who are found to be engaged in this study may be scrutinized by the authorities.

While it is challenging to definitively identify what the risks of conducting these tests may be in your country, we have compiled a number of metrics to help inform you of the potential risks in your country:

These metrics are intended to provide three external sources of information about the general political and legal situation in your country.

BENEFITS

Throughout the 12 month duration of the project you will receive analysis reports of the measurement experiments you have participated in and have the ability to request specific experiments to be run using the measurement software. These reports and custom experiments may help you gain a better understanding of the prevalence, depth, and breadth of Internet filtering in your country.

If you do not consent to the study you may still opt-in to receive aggregated reports summarizing the results of experiments run in regions that are relevant to you, or you may access these aggregated reports on our Web page: www.internetcensorshiplab.org.

There are no other direct benefits from participation in this research study, although your participation may help contribute to the public good by educating society about the prevalence, depth, and breadth of Internet filtering around the world.

CONFIDENTIALITY

Protecting Your Privacy in this Study

We will take steps to help make sure that all the information we get about you is kept private. Your name will not be used wherever possible. We will use a tag (unique alphanumerical user identifier) instead. This tag has been provided to you with the measurement software and should be used to indicate your consent to run the software at the end of this form. All the study data that we get from you will be on a machine in a secured computer network that is only accessible to members of the research team. The code/mapping will be stored in an encrypted database separate from the study results. If any papers and talks are given about this research, neither your name nor your IP address will be used.

Every effort will be made to maintain the strictest confidentiality. Data will only be used for the purposes outlined in the information and consent forms. Only members of the research team will have access to personally identifiable data. No information about research participants, or provided by research participants during the research, will be disclosed to others without the participants’ written consent.

Electronic data will be stored in a secure server environment maintained by the PIs. Any personally identifiable data kept on computers outside of this secure server environment will be encrypted at all times using strong encryption (128 bit AES) except to the extent researchers need to decrypt them during use.

Experiment data may include issuing requests for URLs found in the URL list (similar to what a user does manually with a browser, such as Firefox), DNS queries (mapping hostnames such as Google.com to IP address such as 72.14.52.3), or traceroutes (measuring network paths). Experiments may only log network traffic associated with the experiment (e.g., network packets and timestamps).

Once an experiment has completed the resulting data will be sent, along with the experiment ID and user ID, to the data management system. Once the data management system confirms receipt of the data it will be removed from the client software. Sensitive data that will be collected and transmitted is the IP address of the user. Experiment results will be encrypted to ensure they are not observable in transit and this personally identifiable data (as well as the unique user ID) will only be accessible to researchers at Stony Brook University and the University of Toronto.

Whenever the client communicates with the experiment controller or the data management system the data will be encrypted and may also be sent to a proxy (e.g., in Amazon EC2 data centers) before being forwarded on to the target system. The proxy will not store the data and will not contain the key required to decrypt any data it forwards.
